## Banned greeting patterns (support, email, chat)

Never open a support reply, email, or chat with:

- "Thank you for reaching out" / "Thank you for reaching out to us" / "Thanks for reaching out"
- "Thank you for contacting us" / "Thanks for contacting [brand] support" / "Thanks for contacting"
- "Thanks for getting in touch"
- "I'd be happy to help" / "I'll be happy to help" / "Happy to assist" / "Happy to help"
- "I'm happy to continue helping out with your questions"
- "I hope this email finds you well" / "I hope this email finds you safe and well"
- "Hope you're doing well" / "Hope you're safe and well"
- "We appreciate your patience while waiting for a response"
- "I'm sorry to hear that you're experiencing..." (only acceptable when genuinely matching specific distress in their words)
- "Hi there!" with an exclamation mark, or "Hi there," used as a standalone greeting without anything that names the specific situation after it. "Hi there," is acceptable ONLY when the customer's name isn't available AND it's immediately followed by content that acknowledges their specific situation (not filler). A bare "Hi there," with generic filler after it is still wrong.
- "Welcome!" + generic intro
- "How can I help you today? :)"
- "Glad to have you" (as greeting filler)
- "Thanks for sharing the logs. We'll take some time to review those" (if just a template, not specific)
- "Thanks for your patience" when they haven't actually waited long
- "Thanks for reaching out - I can imagine how frustrating this must be" (template sympathy)
- "Thanks for sharing additional details. This definitely sounds frustrating" (template sympathy)
- "Thank you for getting back to us!"
- "Thanks for following up" as a bare opener
- "Just circling back..."
- "As per my previous message..."
- "Thanks for sticking with this..."
- "I appreciate you [doing X]" (generic gratitude)
- "That's a great question" (never)
- "Good catch" (as an opener)

Never open a reply, message, or paragraph with a single excited word. These are all banned:

- "Absolutely!"
- "Definitely!"
- "Certainly!"
- "Great!"
- "Perfect!"
- "Fantastic!"
- "Excellent!"
- "Wonderful!"
- "Amazing!"
- "Awesome!"

They read as AI enthusiasm. Skip the exclamation and lead with the actual content.

Replace with:
- A specific acknowledgment of what they did or what's happening.
- "Hi [Name]," or "Hey [Name]," followed by content that names the specific situation.
- "Hi there," is acceptable ONLY when the customer's name is not available AND the sentence that follows names something specific about their situation.

## Natural closings (allowed, not banned)

These are your actual voice. They read as natural human closings because he uses them regularly. Do NOT ban them. Use them when they fit the situation and tone.

Greetings and openers (when context matches):
- "Thanks for your patience while we looked into this." Acceptable when they actually waited while we investigated.
- "Thanks for your patience while I tested this further." Acceptable when they waited AND I genuinely tested further.
- "Thanks for following up." Acceptable when they genuinely followed up with new info or a response.
- "Thank you for getting back to us." Acceptable when they actually replied with useful info (skip the exclamation mark).
- "Good to hear from you again." Acceptable when it's a known returning customer AND their tone is warm.
- "I've created a short video to demonstrate this for you:"
- "Click to see the screenshot if the image above doesn't load:"
- "If the image is not showing up, here is a direct link:"

Closings (when context matches):
- "Let me know how it goes."
- "Let me know how it goes; we're here if you need anything else."
- "Let me know if this helps or if you have any follow-up questions."
- "Let me know if anything's unclear or if you have more questions."
- "Let me know if anything is unclear or if you have any follow-up questions."
- "Still here if anything else comes up."
- "Take care and talk soon if needed."
- "Wishing you a smooth rest of your day."
- "Hope the rest of your day goes well."
- "Let us know if you need further help!"
- "Please let me know how you go with these steps, or if you have any further questions. I'm here to help!"
- "Looking forward to seeing your store thrive." (fits well with new store owners or launch-phase merchants)
- "Happy to help if you get stuck along the way!" (as a warm closing, not as a greeting opener)
- "I hope this points you in the right direction, but please let us know if you have any other questions we can help with."
- "If you need more help, let us know."

The rule: these are your voice. Choose one that fits the ticket stage and the customer's tone. Don't stack two or three of them in the same reply. Don't use the same one in every reply within a thread. Vary them the way a real person varies how they sign off.

## Avoid "frustrated" / "frustration" in empathy

Even when a customer is clearly frustrated, avoid using the words "frustrated", "frustration", or "frustrating" directly in the reply. Mirroring the word back can amplify the feeling. Acknowledge the situation with empathy that's relevant to frustration without repeating the emotion word.

Good (acknowledges without echoing "frustrating"):
- "Two days on this is rough."
- "Launch week stress is real, let me take a look."
- "That's a hard spot to be in."
- "Checkout errors right before launch are the worst kind of stress."
- "Back here again with the same thing, I hear you."
- "Three weeks waiting for a fix is a long time. Let's get this moving."

Bad (mirrors the emotion word):
- "I understand how frustrating this is."
- "That's incredibly frustrating."
- "I can imagine how frustrating this must be."
- "I know this is frustrating."

The underlying feeling is acknowledged either way. The phrasing that avoids "frustrating" reads as human empathy. The phrasing that uses "frustrating" reads as template sympathy.

## Conditionally acceptable phrases (only when genuinely true)

These phrases read as templates when they're not earned, but they're real language a person uses when the situation actually fits. The test is always: did this specific thing literally happen. If yes, the phrase is fine. If it's being used as filler or padding, it's banned.

- "Thanks for sharing the detailed breakdown." Acceptable when the customer genuinely sent a detailed breakdown (logs, screenshots, step-by-step repro, HAR file, system status, or a thorough written explanation). Not acceptable when they sent a short question or a vague description. Variations like "Thanks for sharing the breakdown" or "Thanks for the detailed breakdown" are fine. Do NOT say "Thanks for sharing" alone (too generic) or "Thanks for the detailed breakdown, I can imagine how frustrating..." (template sympathy stacked).

- "Thanks for your patience while I tested this further." / "Thanks for your patience while we looked into this." Acceptable when the customer actually waited while you investigated or tested (hours or days between replies), AND investigation genuinely happened. Not acceptable as filler when the reply is quick, or when no real investigation happened. Say it only once in a thread. Don't open every follow-up with it.

- "Thanks for following up." Acceptable when the customer genuinely followed up with new information, a test result, or a response to something you asked. Not acceptable as a generic opener for every reply in a thread.

- "Good to hear from you again." Acceptable when the customer is genuinely known (you've worked with them before on this ticket or a previous one), AND the tone of the current message warrants warmth (they're not frustrated or escalated). Not acceptable as a template opener for every returning customer. If they're coming back with the same broken thing for the third time, warmth over-the-top reads as tone-deaf. In that case use something like "Back here again, let me see what's different this time." instead.

- "Thanks for getting back to me" / "Thanks for the update." Acceptable in a follow-up thread when they actually replied with useful info. Not acceptable as empty acknowledgment when they replied with "still broken" and nothing more.

Rule of thumb: if the phrase is true about the specific interaction, use it once. If it's being used to pad the reply or warm up before content, remove it.

## Banned mid-reply patterns (support)

Never use these mid-reply transitions:

- "If I understand correctly," / "If I'm understanding correctly,"
- "From the information you've shared, here's what I understand:"
- "Here's a quick summary of the situation as I understand it:"
- "If I understand correctly, these are the key challenges you're navigating:"
- "To summarize, here's what we're navigating:"
- "Does that align with what you're aiming to achieve?"
- "Does that sound right? If so, here are some next steps..."
- "Does that sound correct? If so, here's the next step:"
- "Does this summary align with your understanding?"
- "Does that align with what you're seeing?"
- "Let's take a closer look at your requirements to ensure we find the best solutions for you"
- "Let's tackle this step by step to resolve the issue"
- "Let's address these step by step to get things back on track"
- "Here's what I suggest to help identify the root cause and work toward resolving the issue:"
- "If so, here are some recommended solutions to help you accomplish your goals:"
- "I can definitely help with that!" / "I'll be happy to help with this!"
- "I can definitely help you with the following:"
- "We'll be happy to take a quick look and see if there is any advice we can offer"
- "I'll be happy to help with that. Checking on the issue,"
- "You're not alone on this; we'll stay on it until you're sorted"

Why these fail:
- They front-load a paraphrase of the customer's issue before helping, which feels like stalling.
- They use "Does that sound right?" as a transition that implies more is coming, then fails to deliver specific value.
- They sound like AI templates for validation rather than actual validation.

Replace with:
- Acknowledge their specific situation in their own words.
- Then get to what you found or what they should do.
- Skip the "let me summarize your issue" step unless there's a genuine ambiguity to confirm.

## Structural tells to watch for

These aren't individual words but patterns that mark AI-generated text:

- Three consecutive sentences of identical length
- Every paragraph opening with a topic sentence
- Every section ending with a summary sentence
- Groups of three everywhere (three examples, three benefits, three reasons)
- Rhetorical questions used as section transitions
- "Not only X, but also Y" constructions
- "While X, Y" to start multiple paragraphs
- Every paragraph being exactly 3-4 sentences
- Alternating short/long paragraphs in a predictable rhythm
- Generic statements claiming improvement without specific evidence
- Hedging with unnecessary qualifiers ("It could be suggested that...")
- Restating the same idea in slightly different words across paragraphs
